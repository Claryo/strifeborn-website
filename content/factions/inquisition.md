The Inquisition is a faction built on the belief that judgment is not violence — it is clarity. Their doctrine holds that the world is governed by divine principle, that transgression has consequence, and that the purpose of faith is to perceive and enforce those consequences with precision. The Inquisition does not destroy for the sake of destruction. It acts because it understands, or believes it understands, what the truth requires.

When the Fracture occurred on July 16, 2186 and the Chorus Beyond began its invisible work, the Inquisition intensified its rituals and tightened its doctrine. The pressure amplified what the faction already was: a structure of belief that converts uncertainty into certainty, hesitation into action, and doubt into judgment. Under the Chorus, moderation — the willingness to wait, to reconsider, to permit difference — began to feel like failure. Doctrine did not change. But the tolerance for deviation from it collapsed.

The corruption took the shape of revelation. Self-proclaimed saints rose across Inquisition territory, each claiming direct access to divine truth. Their interpretations diverged, but their certainty did not. Communities reorganized around individual voices rather than shared canon. Judgment became personal, then absolute, then indistinguishable from power. Faith that had once been a framework for shared meaning became a competition of convictions, each claiming final authority over all others.

The Inquisition's core fear is not heresy from without, but from within — the moment when belief becomes so certain of itself that it no longer needs truth to sustain it. The Path of Judgment is a record of what happens when faith stops asking questions and starts answering them with finality. In a world where certainty is the enemy, the most dangerous conviction is the one that believes it is beyond doubt.
